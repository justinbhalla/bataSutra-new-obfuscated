UPI Without Borders: India’s Payments Network Scales Abroad

bataSutra | August 09, 2025

UPI has moved from domestic rails to a template for cross-border micro-payments. The sell: cheap, instant settlement at the point of interaction—QR codes, links, or merchant apps—without forcing tourists or diaspora users into expensive card rails. The geopolitics: standards-setting as soft power, where interoperability becomes a foreign policy instrument.

Why This Shift Matters Right Now

Cross-border remittances and tourism spend are ripe for disruption. Travelers want to scan-and-pay; SMEs want instant settlement; regulators want visibility. As real-time payment systems proliferate, who connects whom decides fee flows and data gravity. India’s push positions UPI as a bridge protocol—linking RTP systems, wallets, and banks through standardized APIs and settlement frameworks.

Tourism & Merchants: Acceptance Before Scale

UPI’s international expansion begins where incentives align: airports, malls, hospitality, and attractions with dense Indian footfall. Merchant acquirers care about cost to accept and settlement certainty. Once a core acceptance network is in place, long-tail merchants join via aggregators. The small UX win—no currency confusion at checkout—drives repeat usage and word-of-mouth adoption.

Remittances: Speed, FX, and Compliance

For remittances, three variables decide traction: FX transparency, fee compression, and regulatory clarity. Corridor-by-corridor deals with banks, FMIs, and fintechs determine whether UPI becomes a front-end UX layer or a settlement rail. Expect hybrid models—front-end UPI experience with existing cross-border settlement pipes initially—moving to deeper integration as compliance frameworks mature.

Standards Diplomacy and Data Trust

Exports of standards are stickier than exports of code. The play is to align schemas, authentication, and dispute flows so that UPI apps interoperate with local systems securely. Data trust is the moat: strong consent, tokenization, and auditable logs. Countries will differ on data localization and AML rules; the answer is configurable policy scaffolding rather than one-size-fits-all tech.

The bata takeaway

UPI abroad will grow where adoption is most useful first: tourist hotspots and high-remittance corridors. Over time, interoperability—not branding—will determine who owns the user’s default payment behavior. India’s bet is that open, cheap, and instant wins repeatedly.
