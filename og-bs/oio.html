Ola Electric IPO — The EV Gamble That Could Flip Dalal Street

bataSutra | July 11, 2025

It’s official: Ola Electric is now a public company, and India’s EV story just shifted into overdrive. From Bhavish Aggarwal’s vision to the record-breaking retail oversubscription, this IPO is more than a listing—it’s a stress test for Indian manufacturing, startup dreams, and how much retail India believes in new-age growth.

The IPO Numbers: Mania or Measured?

The ₹7,250 crore issue was over 10x subscribed on day one, with retail bids pouring in like a Diwali sale. But this isn’t Paytm 2021. Institutional investors, anchor funds, and even a clutch of sovereign players lined up. The opening bell saw a 29% listing pop before profit-booking set in.

Retail India is chasing a future on wheels. For the first time, a “new energy” unicorn—once mocked for burning VC cash—is being valued on its manufacturing prowess and scale, not just on topline growth.

Can Ola Deliver Beyond the Hype?

Ola’s EV journey has had speed bumps: delays, recalls, and a still-nascent charging ecosystem. Yet, the FutureFactory in Tamil Nadu is rolling out 1 scooter every 30 seconds, and plans for motorcycles and three-wheelers are live. Ola’s real moat is its integration—from cell manufacturing to software.

The government’s renewed FAME incentives and China’s EV slowdown are tailwinds. But Ola now faces quarterly scrutiny and brutal retail expectations. Can it avoid the “startup-to-stock market” curse that tripped up Paytm, Zomato, and others?

EV Market Reality: Unicorns, Tata, and Global Eyes

India’s EV market is at an inflection point. Tata Motors, Mahindra, and MG are ramping up. Battery localization, rare earth security, and charging infra are still gaps. But with Ola’s IPO, the world is watching if India can leapfrog not just in adoption, but in building a true green supply chain.

Next up? Ather, Exide’s battery arm, and a handful of mobility SaaS startups are prepping IPO dockets. For founders, Ola’s debut could set the template (or warning) for Indian innovation on the bourses.

The bata takeaway

Ola’s IPO is a referendum on “Make in India” 2.0. If it works, expect a flood of climate-tech and mobility unicorns eyeing Dalal Street. If it flops, it could chill India’s retail risk appetite for years. For now, the EV dream has its poster child—and the next quarter’s numbers will be a litmus test for the whole ecosystem.
