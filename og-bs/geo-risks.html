Mitigating Geopolitical Risk in Supply Chains

- bataSutra editorial team

Indian businesses are re-evaluating supplier networks amid rising global tensions.

For decades, globalization promised efficiency. Supply chains stretched across continents, optimized for cost and speed. But in today’s fractured geopolitical climate, that promise is under siege. From the Suez Canal blockage to the Russia-Ukraine war, from Taiwan Strait tensions to shifting U.S.-China trade dynamics, global disruption is no longer the exception—it’s the baseline. And Indian businesses are recalibrating fast.

The End of Just-in-Time?

The traditional ‘just-in-time’ model—designed for efficiency and minimal inventory—has come under scrutiny. In a world where a factory shutdown in China can delay a shipment to Chennai, or a Red Sea missile strike can spike freight costs overnight, lean inventories can quickly become costly liabilities.

Indian companies are recognizing the need for a buffer. Many are now exploring ‘just-in-case’ strategies—stockpiling critical components, building regional redundancies, and nurturing multi-supplier ecosystems. Warehousing is getting decentralized. Logistics partners are being diversified. And scenario planning is becoming a board-level conversation.

China+1: A Strategic Realignment

One of the most visible shifts is the embrace of the "China+1" strategy. While China remains a vital node in the global supply web, Indian firms are actively seeking alternative sourcing and manufacturing destinations—Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, even Eastern Europe and Latin America. This is not just about political tensions. Rising wages, stricter compliance requirements, and environmental constraints are also nudging companies to look elsewhere.

At home, this is translating into investments in backward integration. Manufacturers are reassessing where they get their semiconductors, APIs, rare earth materials, or even basic electronics. The broader goal: reduce overdependence on single-country risk, especially where diplomatic and defense tensions run high.

Policy Tailwinds: The Role of the Indian State

The Indian government has stepped in with deliberate intent. The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes, aimed at boosting domestic manufacturing across electronics, pharma, textiles, and automotive, are already beginning to shape sourcing decisions. With over ₹2 lakh crore earmarked across sectors, PLI isn’t just a fiscal incentive—it’s a geopolitical strategy.

Other interventions—like infrastructure upgrades under the Gati Shakti plan, trade agreements with nations like the UAE and Australia, and active participation in supply chain dialogues like the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework (IPEF)—are strengthening India’s position as a credible, stable hub in global networks.

Geopolitical Intelligence as a Business Function

Enterprises are no longer flying blind. A new discipline is taking root inside Indian boardrooms: geopolitical risk intelligence. Companies are hiring regional risk analysts, subscribing to intelligence feeds, and using scenario modeling tools powered by AI and satellite data. Multinationals with Indian operations are embedding geopolitical risk matrices into their procurement scorecards.

This isn’t academic. Consider a company reliant on rare earth minerals from Myanmar, or semiconductors from Taiwan. A coup or a naval blockade can bring operations to a halt. Businesses are learning to model these risks—assigning probabilities, planning contingencies, and even budgeting for relocation or substitution.

Digital Twins, Blockchain, and Predictive Resilience

Technology is the new backbone of resilient supply chains. Digital twins—virtual replicas of physical supply networks—allow companies to test what-if scenarios in real time. What if a supplier in Guangdong goes offline? What if airspace over Europe is restricted? These simulations enable faster, more informed decisions before a crisis strikes.

Blockchain is being explored to trace the origin and movement of goods, especially in high-risk or regulated industries like defense manufacturing or pharma. Predictive analytics, powered by global news data, logistics feeds, and IoT, are helping businesses preempt disruptions rather than react to them.

Beyond Cost: A Shift in Procurement Philosophy

Perhaps the most fundamental change is philosophical. Procurement leaders are being asked to look beyond cost savings and think like strategists. Risk diversification, ethical sourcing, ESG compliance, and policy alignment are all new dimensions of supplier selection. In this context, Tier-2 and Tier-3 suppliers are also being assessed not just for quality, but for geopolitical exposure.

In automotive, for instance, OEMs are mapping the full supply web down to raw materials. In aerospace and defense, dual-use component regulations are forcing firms to scrutinize origin, licensing, and export control history. In pharmaceuticals, APIs from China are being reevaluated in favor of India or the EU—even at higher costs.

Case Studies: How Indian Firms Are Responding

  • Tata Electronics is building semiconductor and precision electronics capabilities in Hosur, aimed at reducing chip import dependency.
  • Sun Pharma has diversified its API sourcing footprint beyond China, investing in in-house R&D and manufacturing in Gujarat and Madhya Pradesh.
  • Larsen & Toubro has started leveraging digital twins for its complex EPC (engineering, procurement, construction) supply networks, improving responsiveness to regional risk scenarios.
  • Maruti Suzuki is investing in local supplier development to ensure EV components are available within India, minimizing reliance on volatile import corridors.

The Road Ahead: Sovereignty Meets Supply Chain

India’s aspirations to become a global manufacturing leader—whether in semiconductors, EVs, or precision machinery—hinge not just on scale, but on strategic autonomy. Supply chains must be viewed through the lens of sovereignty, security, and long-term competitiveness.

This calls for a radical rethinking. Businesses must invest in strategic inventory buffers, dual-sourcing frameworks, local ecosystem building, and embedded risk management capabilities. Universities and think tanks need to step in to develop India’s own cadre of geopolitical supply chain experts. And public-private collaboration must deepen, especially in sectors deemed critical to national interest.

Geopolitical risk is no longer a tail event—it’s a structural variable. The sooner Indian businesses embrace this reality, the stronger and more shockproof their global ambitions will become.
