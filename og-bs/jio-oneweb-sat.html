Jio, OneWeb, and the Great Indian Satellite Internet Leap: Why LEOs Could Rewrite Rural Connectivity

bataSutra | July 07, 2025

For decades, the “digital divide” in India has been as wide as the Ganges. 4G and fiber rollouts promised a broadband revolution, yet 450+ million Indians remain on the wrong side of the internet tracks—underserved, offline, or at best, limping on 2G. Now, with the arrival of satellite broadband from the likes of Jio and OneWeb, the equation may be changing at light speed—literally.

The LEO Opportunity: What’s Different This Time?

Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite constellations orbit just 500-2,000km above Earth, unlike older, slower geostationary satellites. This means much lower latency, better speeds, and—crucially—reliable internet beamed directly to remote villages, mountain hamlets, border outposts, and isolated islands. The “last mile” is no longer about digging expensive fiber trenches or waiting years for mobile towers.

JioSpaceFiber launched its first live pilots in late 2024, targeting select gram panchayats in Gujarat and Uttarakhand. Bharti-backed OneWeb (now part of Eutelsat) isn’t far behind, promising pan-India LEO broadband by mid-2025. Elon Musk’s Starlink remains stuck in regulatory limbo, but has signaled intent with test kits and rural demonstrations.

The Business Model: Can It Scale?

Satellite broadband isn’t cheap—yet. Pilot tariffs hover around ₹900-₹1,200 per month for rural institutions (schools, health centers), and ₹2,000-₹3,000 for SMEs and small offices. Jio is betting on scale, subsidies, and government partnerships (think Digital India, BharatNet) to bring costs down for households. OneWeb is positioning itself as the “carrier’s carrier”—offering backhaul and enterprise links for telcos, with retail rollout left to local ISPs.

The economics improve dramatically for “shared connectivity”: a single rooftop dish can serve dozens via local WiFi or mesh networks, bringing real 100 Mbps broadband to villages for the first time. It’s also an insurance policy for remote offices, ATMs, and even disaster zones.

Regulatory Roulette: Why India’s Space Policy Matters

The real battle, though, is regulatory. The Indian government wants control—over spectrum, ground stations, data flows, and foreign ownership. The new Indian Space Policy (2023) opened the doors to private satellite operators, but the satcom licensing process remains bureaucratic and fraught with delays. Jio has ISRO on its side, but Starlink and OneWeb still face licensing headwinds, with questions over national security and lawful interception.

Why Is This Moment Different?

1. Tech is finally ready. Antenna hardware is compact and cheap enough for rooftop installation, and LEO constellations offer true broadband speeds (unlike the VSAT of old).

2. Digital demand is surging. Government schemes—from ONDC for ecommerce to telemedicine and digital banking—are now mission-critical in rural India.

3. A true public-private play. Jio, OneWeb, and ISRO are collaborating with government departments and state agencies. The next wave of BharatNet could be space-powered.

The bata takeaway

For the first time in decades, India’s digital divide could shrink not from the ground up, but from the sky down. The race isn’t just between Jio and OneWeb—it's between a 20th-century model of “wait and dig” and a 21st-century leapfrog. If the business model, tech, and policy stars align, LEO broadband could redefine what it means to be “connected”—for every Indian.
