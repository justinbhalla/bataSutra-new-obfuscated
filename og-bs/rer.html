Real Estate Revival? Why Commercial Realty Is Back in Play

Office spaces are having a moment. After a turbulent pandemic-era freeze and a sluggish recovery through 2023, India’s commercial real estate market is showing signs of a sharp rebound. Investors are taking note — and so are corporate tenants.

What’s Driving the Rebound?

  • Leasing demand surges: Q1 2025 saw a 19% YoY jump in gross office leasing across India’s top 7 cities, led by Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Pune.
  • Hybrid settles into steady state: The "3-2" hybrid work model is pushing firms to expand flexible workspace hubs instead of giving up leases entirely.
  • Global capability centers (GCCs): New GCC setups from US and EU firms are accelerating Grade A office absorption — especially in South India.

Follow the Capital

Private equity flows into commercial realty hit $1.6B in Q1, up from $900M in the same period last year. Brookfield, Blackstone, and GIC are doubling down on IT parks and logistics hubs. Indian REITs, too, are drawing record retail inflows — a signal that real estate is re-entering investor portfolios in earnest.

The REIT Factor

India now has 5 listed REITs, and they’re gaining traction with younger retail investors. Why? Predictable rental yields, quarterly distributions, and exposure to institutional-grade real estate — without the capital lock-in of physical property. This financialization of real estate is reshaping how urban India invests.

Challenges Remain

  • Oversupply risk: Speculative development in Tier-1 peripheries could drag down yields if demand flattens.
  • Financing headwinds: Rising interest rates globally are still weighing on capex-heavy realty bets.
  • Policy bottlenecks: Despite RERA, approval delays and land aggregation challenges continue to haunt developers.

The Bottom Line

Commercial real estate isn’t just recovering — it’s evolving. From traditional leases to flex spaces, from builders to REITs, the market is becoming deeper, more liquid, and more investor-friendly. Whether the momentum sustains will depend on macroeconomic stability and how India’s corporate work culture adapts in 2025 and beyond.
