The New Indian Credit Revolution: UPI, BNPL, and Why the Next Decade Belongs to Digital Borrowers

bataSutra | July 07, 2025

For generations, Indian consumers have tiptoed around formal credit. Credit cards barely scratch 6% penetration. Loans meant endless paperwork and waiting. But in the last three years, digital lending has exploded, with UPI credit and Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) unlocking new ways for urban and semi-urban Indians to borrow, spend, and build financial histories.

The UPI Credit Phenomenon: “Tap, Swipe, Borrow”

The RBI’s greenlight for UPI credit lines in 2024 was a game-changer. Suddenly, every UPI QR code at kirana shops and malls became a credit point. PhonePe, Paytm, and Google Pay have rolled out instant pre-approved credit lines linked directly to UPI. There’s no need for a physical card—users get a sanctioned limit, tap to pay, and repay via UPI in easy installments.

For banks, it’s a lifeline: new customers and transaction volumes without the cost of plastic issuance or merchant onboarding. For fintechs, it’s a gateway to lending at scale, with AI-driven risk models and real-time approval. For users, it’s financial inclusion—no more “credit denied” at checkout.

BNPL 2.0: Beyond Quick Loans, Towards Embedded Finance

BNPL players like ZestMoney, Simpl, and LazyPay have evolved from micro-loans to full-fledged checkout finance and subscription plans. Amazon Pay and Flipkart Pay Later have made installment payments the norm for everything from groceries to smartphones.

The new BNPL isn’t just “split payments”—it’s an ecosystem. Travel, insurance, and edtech platforms now embed lending offers at the point of purchase, often with zero paperwork and instant approval. This is driving the next wave of consumption, especially among salaried millennials and young professionals.

Banks, Fintechs, and the Regulatory Gauntlet

Legacy lenders aren’t backing down. HDFC, ICICI, and SBI have slashed fees and are partnering with fintechs to push virtual cards and co-branded credit lines. But the RBI’s digital lending guidelines are tightening—forcing stricter KYC, capping interest rates, and banning “rent-a-license” shadow lending models.

The result? Cleaner, more transparent lending—but also a squeeze on unprofitable, high-risk BNPL models. Only the biggest, best-capitalized fintechs are likely to survive the next regulatory cull.

Risks and Rewards: Is Credit Getting Too Easy?

There’s a flipside. Consumer credit scores are rising, but so is household debt, especially in cities. Fintechs and banks are investing heavily in data science to detect early delinquencies and nudge users towards healthy repayment behavior. The RBI is watching the space closely, wary of a “mini-NPA” crisis among first-time borrowers.

The bata takeaway

In the next decade, India’s “credit culture” will be shaped by mobile-first, digital lending—paperless, instant, and deeply embedded in daily commerce. The winners will be those who blend trust, transparency, and tech-driven risk controls. The new Indian borrower isn’t just urban or wealthy—they’re young, mobile, and ready to swipe, tap, and borrow their way into a new middle class.
